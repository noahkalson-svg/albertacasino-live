Alberta's self-exclusion system explained - and why it's better than Ontario's

June 14, 2026 5 min read

One of the most important player protections built into Alberta's new iGaming framework is also one of the least talked about: a centralised self-exclusion system that covers every licensed operator from the very first day the market opens.

At launch on July 13, system-wide self-exclusion and financial and time-based limit tools will be available to Albertans. Licensed operators will also be required to provide players with activity statements and take action when signs of problem gambling are identified. All operators must integrate their platforms with the AGLC's centralised self-exclusion system before going live - it's not optional.

The short version: If you register for self-exclusion in Alberta, every single AGLC-licensed casino and sportsbook is blocked simultaneously - including land-based casinos. You only have to register once.

Why this matters - the Ontario comparison

In Ontario - Canada's first regulated iGaming province - centralised self-exclusion was not available at launch in 2022. Players who wanted to exclude themselves had to do so operator by operator, a process that was easy to circumvent simply by signing up with a different brand.

Alberta learned from that experience and built centralised self-exclusion into the framework from the start - it's written directly into the AGLC's operator requirements as a go-live condition, not an afterthought.

How to use Alberta's self-exclusion system

From July 13, here's how the process will work:

Visit the AGLC's website and complete the self-exclusion form. You'll need to verify your identity. The process takes around 10 minutes.
2

Options typically include 3 months, 6 months, 1 year, 3 years, or permanent exclusion. You can always extend - but you cannot shorten an exclusion period once it's set.

Every AGLC-registered operator is notified and must block your account. This happens automatically - you don't need to contact each operator separately.
4
Land-based casinos are also covered
Unlike Ontario, Alberta's system covers both online and in-person gambling venues from a single registration.

Other mandatory player protections at launch

Beyond self-exclusion, every AGLC-licensed operator must have these tools active from day one:

Deposit limits - you can set daily, weekly, or monthly deposit caps. Operators cannot allow you to exceed them.

Time-out periods - short breaks of 24 hours to 30 days, initiated instantly from your account settings.

Cooling-off periods - longer pauses (up to 6 weeks) that prevent you from reversing the decision in a moment of impulse.

Activity statements - regular summaries of your deposits, withdrawals, wins, and losses. Operators must provide these and must reach out if your patterns suggest problem gambling.

The Alberta iGaming Corporation has also made RG Check accreditation - a certification from the Responsible Gambling Council - a mandatory requirement for every operator, not just a goal. It must be maintained in good standing while they operate in the province.

The bottom line

Alberta's player protection framework is genuinely stronger than what Ontario launched with. If you're considering playing at a regulated site for the first time when the market opens, the tools are there to keep it controlled. And if you ever need to step back, the self-exclusion process from July 13 will be simple: one registration, all platforms, both online and in person.

Alberta's casino bonus advertising ban explained - and how players can still claim welcome offers

June 24, 2026 5 min read Updated June 24

If you were expecting Alberta's July 13 launch to look like Ontario's - wall-to-wall TV ads, every sports broadcast interrupted by welcome bonus offers, celebrity endorsers holding up their phones - you are going to be surprised. Alberta's regulator has drawn a significantly harder line on bonus advertising than any other regulated market in Canada, and understanding what that means is essential for any player planning to sign up after launch day.

On June 18, the Alberta Gaming, Liquor and Cannabis Commission published an update to its Standards and Requirements for iGaming that formalised advertising rules across the board. The rules had been telegraphed for months, but the June 18 document locked them in with weeks to go before launch. The short version: public bonus advertising is banned. But bonuses themselves are not.

The key distinction: Operators cannot publicly advertise bonuses, free bets, or gambling inducements. But they can still offer them - you just need to be opted in to direct marketing or visit the operator's own site to find them. Welcome offers are not going away. The way you find them is changing.

What the AGLC has banned

The June 18 update prohibits operators from advertising gambling inducements, bonuses, and credits anywhere except on their own gaming site or through direct marketing - and only after a player has explicitly opted in. In practice, this bans:

Banned from July 13

  • TV, radio, and billboard ads mentioning bonuses or free bets
  • Sponsored social media posts promoting welcome offers
  • Influencer or celebrity endorsements referencing bonuses
  • Google or Meta ads promoting bonus offers to general audiences
  • Affiliate sites displaying bonus offers without player opt-in
  • Promotions featuring athletes, celebrities, or cartoon characters
  • Any ad that could appeal to minors

Still permitted

  • Brand awareness advertising (operator name and logo only)
  • Bonus offers displayed on the operator's own website or app
  • Direct email, text, or social media messages to opted-in players
  • Promotions on the operator's own social channels to followers
  • Operator-run draws, prizes, and giveaways on their own platform
  • Welcome offers for players who register and opt in to marketing

The AGLC also reserves the right to step in at any time and tell an operator to adjust or end a promotion in the name of upholding social responsibility or the integrity of gaming. Third-party marketers working with licensed operators cannot promote or refer players to unregistered gambling sites.

How Alberta's rules compare to Ontario

Ontario's advertising rules, introduced after significant criticism of aggressive bonus advertising at launch in 2022, are broadly similar - but Alberta is going further in a few meaningful ways. Ontario implemented its bonus advertising restrictions in 2023, a year after launch, after public and political pressure over the volume of gambling ads. Alberta is baking the restrictions in from day one.

The celebrity and athlete advertising ban is also more strictly enforced in Alberta. Ontario has had issues with operators using active professional athletes in promotional material - something that has been widely cited as particularly problematic for its appeal to younger audiences. How live baccarat works

Before the round begins, you place your bet - Banker, Player, or Tie. The dealer then draws two cards each for the Banker and Player hands. Hand values are calculated by adding card values together and taking the rightmost digit: a 7 and 8 = 15, so the hand value is 5. The hand closest to 9 wins.

You don't need to memorise when third cards are drawn - the live dealer manages this according to the fixed drawing rules. Your only decision is which bet to place before each round.
