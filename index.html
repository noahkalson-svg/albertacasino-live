Alberta's self-exclusion system explained - and why it's better than Ontario's

June 14, 2026 5 min read

One of the most important player protections built into Alberta's new iGaming framework is also one of the least talked about: a centralised self-exclusion system that covers every licensed operator from the very first day the market opens.

At launch on July 13, system-wide self-exclusion and financial and time-based limit tools will be available to Albertans. Licensed operators will also be required to provide players with activity statements and take action when signs of problem gambling are identified. All operators must integrate their platforms with the AGLC's centralised self-exclusion system before going live - it's not optional.

The short version: If you register for self-exclusion in Alberta, every single AGLC-licensed casino and sportsbook is blocked simultaneously - including land-based casinos. You only have to register once.

Why this matters - the Ontario comparison

In Ontario - Canada's first regulated iGaming province - centralised self-exclusion was not available at launch in 2022. Players who wanted to exclude themselves had to do so operator by operator, a process that was easy to circumvent simply by signing up with a different brand.

Ontario's iGaming regulator has publicly acknowledged the gap, with its CEO describing the situation as "overdue." Alberta learned from that experience and built centralised self-exclusion into the framework from the start - it's written directly into the AGLC's operator requirements as a go-live condition, not an afterthought.

How to use Alberta's self-exclusion system

From July 13, here's how the process will work:

1
Register at the AGLC self-exclusion portal
Visit the AGLC's website and complete the self-exclusion form. You'll need to verify your identity. The process takes around 10 minutes.
2
Choose your exclusion period
Options typically include 3 months, 6 months, 1 year, 3 years, or permanent exclusion. You can always extend - but you cannot shorten an exclusion period once it's set.
3
Exclusion is applied across all platforms
Every AGLC-registered operator is notified and must block your account. This happens automatically - you don't need to contact each operator separately.
4
Land-based casinos are also covered
Unlike Ontario, Alberta's system covers both online and in-person gambling venues from a single registration.

Other mandatory player protections at launch

Beyond self-exclusion, every AGLC-licensed operator must have these tools active from day one:

Deposit limits - you can set daily, weekly, or monthly deposit caps. Operators cannot allow you to exceed them.

Time-out periods - short breaks of 24 hours to 30 days, initiated instantly from your account settings.

Cooling-off periods - longer pauses (up to 6 weeks) that prevent you from reversing the decision in a moment of impulse.

Activity statements - regular summaries of your deposits, withdrawals, wins, and losses. Operators must provide these and must reach out if your patterns suggest problem gambling.

The Alberta iGaming Corporation has also made RG Check accreditation - a certification from the Responsible Gambling Council - a mandatory requirement for every operator, not just a goal. It must be maintained in good standing while they operate in the province.

The bottom line

Alberta's player protection framework is genuinely stronger than what Ontario launched with. If you're considering playing at a regulated site for the first time when the market opens, the tools are there to keep it controlled. And if you ever need to step back, the self-exclusion process from July 13 will be simple: one registration, all platforms, both online and in person.

Regulation Update

Alberta missed the World Cup window - but July 13 is still a landmark date

June 12, 2026 3 min read

The 2026 FIFA World Cup kicked off this week across Canada, the US, and Mexico - and Alberta's regulated casino and sports betting market won't be live for it. That timing has drawn criticism from industry insiders, and it's worth understanding what happened, why it matters, and what players should actually expect on July 13.

Bottom line for players: July 13 is still the date to bookmark. The World Cup final is July 19 - six days after launch - and the NHL and NFL seasons follow in the autumn. The best commercial moment for Alberta's market is still ahead of it.

What the industry is saying

The new regulated Alberta market launches on July 13 - just six days before the World Cup final on July 19. Industry veterans have argued the province missed a significant opportunity by not going live earlier, which would have allowed licensed operators to compete with offshore sites during the tournament's peak betting weeks.

The frustration is understandable from an operator's perspective. The World Cup, held across three North American host nations this year, is the single biggest global sports betting event. Operators who have paid $50,000 in application fees and $150,000 in annual registration costs to enter Alberta were watching from the sidelines while unregulated offshore sites absorbed that betting volume unrestricted.

Why the delay happened

The bottleneck wasn't a lack of interest - 43 operators are now registered, with strong demand from across the industry. The delay was driven by the compliance process itself, particularly the requirement for every operator to integrate their platform with the AGLC's centralised self-exclusion system before going live. That integration takes time, and the AGLC prioritised getting it right over hitting a sporting event deadline.

Alberta's Minister of Service Dale Nally initially wanted to launch before summer. The AGLC has since confirmed it may grant extensions of up to three months - until October 13 - for operators demonstrating a path to compliance that was genuinely unachievable before the initial deadline.

What hasn't been missed

The World Cup final landing six days after launch is not nothing - it's a significant event that licensed Alberta operators will now be able to market around. More importantly, the NHL and NFL regular seasons both begin in October, historically the biggest Canadian sports betting periods of the year. Alberta's market will be fully operational, with a growing roster of operators, well before either league tips off.

On the revenue side, the province's goals are long-term. Alberta retains 20% of iGaming gross gaming revenue, with 2% allocated to First Nations and 1% to responsible gambling research and treatment. The province's aim has never been to maximise a single tournament - it's to redirect the billions flowing to offshore platforms into a regulated system that keeps money and protections in Alberta.
